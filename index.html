New York City gunfire, 2006–2026

Every shooting the police logged in two decades — 24,127 of them — placed, dated and tallied. The arc is not one story but several: a long decline, a pandemic shock, a recovery. The question this page keeps testing is how much actually changed, and where.

Gun violence within serious violence

A shrinking share of serious violence involves a gun

A clarification first: shootings are not a standalone crime category. In the NYPD's seven "major felonies," a shooting is usually folded into one of two buckets — a victim who survives is generally recorded as a felony assault, one who dies as a murder. So shootings are best read as the gun-injury slice of serious violence, not a separate crime running parallel to it. That is why the two are charted at their true, very different scales below — felony assault is an order of magnitude larger — rather than on a shared line. The telling number is the relationship between them: for every 100 felony assaults in 2006 there were about shootings; by 2025, about .
Felony-assault counts are all felony assaults citywide (NYPD complaint data, dated by report date), the great majority of which do not involve a gun. The "shootings per 100 felony assaults" ratio is a rough index of how gun-driven serious violence is — not a clean subset share, since the ~16% of shootings that are fatal are counted as murders, not assaults.

Streetlights & gunfire

Do broken streetlights track the shootings?

A common intuition — dark blocks breed crime — predicts that the city's worst shooting blocks should also be where streetlights stay broken. To test it, every block (a ~110-metre grid cell) was scored on two decades of shootings and on "street light out" complaints to 311 (2010–2026). The two barely line up. Across all shooting blocks the relationship is essentially flat (correlation ), and of the worst shooting blocks only — about — also rank among the city's most-complained-about for outages, slightly below what chance alone would give.
Each dot is a high-shooting block · outage complaints vs shootings

The blocks that are both

the minority of hotspots that are also chronic-outage blocks
A crucial caveat that cuts the other way: 311 counts reported outages, and reporting is uneven — lower-income, higher-violence neighborhoods tend to file fewer 311 complaints per actual problem. So a low outage count on a shooting block may reflect under-reporting, not working lights. This compares where outages are logged, not necessarily where lights are dark. Both layers also rise with population. Treat it as descriptive, not causal.

Methodology, sources & limitations

Sources. NYC Open Data's shooting collection (published Feb 2026): Shootings (5ucz-vwe8), Victims (pztn-9bne), Offenders (gdk4-mbsv), and the shoreline-clipped Police Precincts (y76i-bdw7). Records run ; 2026 is partial.

Counting. "Shootings" are incidents (one row per event); victims/offenders join on incident_key. An incident is fatal if any victim carries the statistical-murder flag. The source latitude/longitude columns are swapped; we correct them before mapping. Junk ages (e.g. 1022) and mixed sex labels are normalized.

Neighborhoods & change windows. The geographic unit is the NYPD precinct, labeled with the neighborhood it mainly covers (approximate, for readability). Share of city total compares a pre-pandemic window (2015–2019 average) with a recent one (2021–2025). Pandemic spikes compare 2018–2019 with 2020–2021. Declines compare 2020–2021 with 2023–2025. The share comparison is also user-selectable to any two years (2006 onward). Low-volume precincts (under 60 lifetime shootings) are excluded from the change tables for stability.

Population & rates. Per-capita rates use actual precinct population in each year, not a fixed figure. We took total population by census tract from the 2010 and 2020 decennial censuses, assigned each tract to a precinct by its centroid, summed, and interpolated between (and extrapolated beyond) the two censuses. This captures real local change — the Financial District precinct grew ~130% and Downtown Brooklyn ~70% between censuses. The all-precinct 2020 total reconciles to ~8.77M. Central Park's precinct is omitted from rates (almost no residents).

Confidence. High for incident counts, geography, timing and victim demographics; medium for offender demographics (identified-suspect subset) and the demographic-share trends (category definitions can shift over twenty years). Nothing is modeled — every figure is a direct count of published records.

How this was made. The pipeline, maps and charts were built with AI assistance (Claude). Every number is computed directly from the four datasets by a transparent script — nothing is AI-estimated. Checks: incident/victim/offender counts reconciled with source totals (24,127 / 28,912 / 18,901); the lat/lon swap caught and corrected against sample records and the precinct map; yearly totals and the fatal-incident join spot-checked; precinct change windows hand-verified for several neighborhoods. Not independently re-reported: NYPD classification, geocoding and arrest decisions, taken as published.
Source: NYPD via NYC Open Data · an independent civic-data project, not affiliated with the NYPD or the City of New York.
