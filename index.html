New York City · 2006–2026

Two decades of gun violence

More than 24,000 shootings, mapped and counted across twenty years — the long decline of the 2010s, the pandemic surge, and the steep fall now under way. Explore where, when and whom.

Every shooting incident the New York Police Department has logged since 2006 is here, joined to records on the people shot and, where an arrest was made, the people who fired. The picture is not one story but several: a citywide drop of more than half through the 2010s, a sharp reversal in 2020, and — over the last four years — a return toward the lowest levels on record.

Methodology, sources and limitations

Data sources. NYC Open Data's shooting collection, published February 2026: Shootings (2006–Present) (5ucz-vwe8), Shooting Victims (pztn-9bne) and Shooting Offenders (gdk4-mbsv). Precinct boundaries are the shoreline-clipped Police Precincts layer (y76i-bdw7). Data pulled ; records run .

What a row is. The Shootings table is incident-level (one row per shooting event). Victims and offenders are joined to incidents on incident_key; a single incident can have several of each. Charts of "shootings" count incidents; demographic charts count victim or offender records. An incident is treated as fatal if at least one of its victims is flagged a statistical murder.

A data fix worth noting. In the source Shootings table the latitude and longitude columns are swapped (the field labeled latitude actually holds longitude). We correct the orientation before mapping; without the fix every point lands in the wrong hemisphere. One record lacks usable coordinates and is mapped nowhere but still counted in every chart.

The precinct map. The choropleth colors each precinct by its shooting count for the selected year (or all years). Counts are not population-adjusted — a busy precinct can lead on raw numbers partly because more people live or move through it. Read it as "where shootings occur," not "your personal risk." 2026 is partial (through March) and will look low.

Offender data caveat. Offender records exist only when a suspect was identified, which happens in a minority of shootings and is itself uneven across place and time. Offender demographics therefore describe identified shooters, an arrest-skewed subset — not all shooters. Victim data, by contrast, covers essentially every shooting. Confidence: high for incident counts, geography and victim demographics; medium for offender demographics.

Other limits. NYPD records reflect reported and recorded shootings; definitions and reporting practices can shift over years. Race/ethnicity categories are the department's own. Nothing here is modeled or projected — every figure is a direct count of published records.

How this was made. The data pipeline, map and charts were built with AI assistance (Claude). Every number is computed directly from the four NYC Open Data datasets above by a transparent script — nothing is AI-estimated or invented. Checks performed: incident, victim and offender row counts reconciled against the source totals (24,127 / 28,912 / 18,901); the latitude/longitude swap was caught and corrected against sample records and the precinct map; the fatal-incident join and yearly totals were spot-checked against the source. Not independently re-reported: the NYPD's own classification, geocoding and arrest decisions, which we take as published. Treat the linked datasets as ground truth.
